Talking about disadvantage
in public narratives

To examine how narratives about disadvantage are presented in the Australian media, a team of Monash University researchers analysed more than two hundred articles from 2020 and further explored whether these narratives differ across different types of disadvantaged groups, issues and publishers. The dataset was first human-coded – meaning that experts analysed the articles to look for emerging themes, keywords and biases – which may help in developing machine learning models to track public narratives using artificial intelligence.

    The most-commonly discussed disadvantaged groups were strong First Nations people (103 articles), women (84 articles), racial minorities (42 articles), children (35 articles), and unemployed people (13.45%).

    Through a qualitative thematic synthesis of all the group labels mentioned, we generated a list of 26 groups that captured all of those discussed as disadvantaged in the included articles. We organised these groups under seven broad categories: cultural minorities, gender and sexuality, age, socioeconomic status, health status, institutionalisation, and other.

    The warm color block represents the code for the Issue. Issues relevant to disadvantage were broadly defined as being the things that articles considered disadvantaged groups to be deprived of or pursuing. These could include, for example, specific material things (e.g. shelter, food), opportunities (e.g. work, education), or broader rights and concepts (e.g. human rights, social inclusion).

    The most commonly-discussed specific issues were violence, work, crime and health. The most commonly-discussed general issues were prejudice and discrimination, followed by inequality, poverty, and human rights. 

    Qualitative thematic analysis revealed that articles discussed both issues relating to specific forms of deprivation (e.g. violence, work, health)(Bessell, 2015), and more general issues (e.g. prejudice and discrimination, human rights).

    Next, let's discuss what are the characteristics of the narrative? We used a statistical tool called factor analysis to examine whether there were underlying broad narrative dimensions being captured by the 18 specific binary indicators we coded on. There are five main characteriscs:

  • "Amplifies disadvantaged voices "
  • "Disparages disadvantaged people"
  • "Recognises intersectionality "
  • "Provides human examples"
  • "Focused on solutions "

    • We generated composite scores for each of the five factors by averaging the relevant binary indicators.
    For example: The binary indicator for "Amplifies disadvantaged voices" characteristic includs:
  • Amplifies disadvantaged voices
  • Describes ideas for positive change coming from disadvantaged groups/ communities
  • ...

    • Each article has a score for five characteristics, and we plot these scores by different publishers.

    Then, we split these points by five characteristics.

    Then show the average score stacked by publisher. This chart shows the extent to which articles had each of the five narrative characteristics. For simplicity, we can say that about half of articles amplified disadvantaged voices, about 16% disparage disadvantaged people, about 35% reconogise intersectionality, about 36% provide human examples, and roughly 60% focus on solutions.
