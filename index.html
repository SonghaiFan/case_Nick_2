Talking about disadvantage
in public narratives

To examine how narratives about disadvantage are presented in the Australian media, a team of Monash University researchers analysed more than two hundred articles from 2020 and further explored whether these narratives differ across different types of disadvantaged groups, issues and publishers. The dataset was first human-coded – meaning that experts analysed the articles to look for emerging themes, keywords and biases – which may help in developing machine learning models to track public narratives using artificial intelligence.

How we collect data and where are they come from?

We searched the Factiva database of Australian news articles for articles related to disadvantage. For example, this article contains key words related to themes of disadvantage highlighted in different colours.

Factiva is a business information and research tool owned by Dow Jones & Company. Factiva aggregates content from both licensed and free sources, and provides organizations with search, alerting, dissemination, and other information management capabilities. Wikipedia

We searched the Factiva database of Australian news articles for articles related to disadvantage. For example, this article contains key words related to themes of disadvantage highlighted in different colours. In this article, "domestic" and "violence" is highlighted, indicate this article is coded with issues about family relationship and violence.

There are two publishers."Fairfax" is an abbreviation for "Fairfax Media Management Pty Limited" and "News" is an abbreviation for "News Ltd.". Other publishers include "Federal Capital Press of Australia Pty Ltd", and "West Australian Newspapers Limited", which is comparatively lesser than these two major publishers, is therefore categorized as simply "Others".

A total of 3845 articles like these were returned after we searched the database for all articles including relevant terms (e.g. disadvantage* OR discrimination OR "access to resources" …) The top 1000 most 'Relevant' article were downloaded and randomised for screening and coding. Human coders screened articles (n=588),And extracted data from those that met inclusion criteria (n=265), then coding stopped.

You can hover over the bar chart to see the articles related to that code, along with the percentage share and specific counts.

To increase the clarity, the plot included only groups and issues that were mentioned at least 25 times in the full dataset. If the article happens to be about only the groups and issues that were filtered out, then the article will also be excluded.

The cold color block represents the code for the Group.

The most-commonly discussed disadvantaged groups were First Nations people (103 articles), women (84 articles), racial minorities (42 articles), children (35 articles), and unemployed people (13.45%).

Through a qualitative thematic synthesis of all the group labels mentioned, we generated a list of 26 groups that captured all of those discussed as disadvantaged in the included articles. We organised these groups under seven broad categories: cultural minorities, gender and sexuality, age, socioeconomic status, health status, institutionalisation, and other.

The warm color block represents the code for the Issue.

The most commonly-discussed specific issues were violence, work, crime and health. The most commonly-discussed general issues were prejudice and discrimination, followed by inequality, poverty, and human rights. 

Qualitative thematic analysis revealed that articles discussed both issues relating to specific forms of deprivation (e.g. violence, work, health)(Bessell, 2015), and more general issues (e.g. prejudice and discrimination, human rights).

We are interested in how different groups of people are discussed in relation to different issues related to disadvantage.
